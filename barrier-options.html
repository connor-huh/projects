An Overview of Barrier Options

Barrier options are path-dependent derivatives: their payoff depends not only on the price of the underlying asset at maturity, but also on whether the asset‚Äôs path crosses a specified level (the ‚Äúbarrier‚Äù) during the life of the option.

A classic example is a down-and-out call. It behaves like a standard European call option unless the asset price falls below a certain barrier during the option‚Äôs term, in which case the option immediately becomes worthless. These structures are particularly common in foreign exchange (FX) markets due to their cost-effectiveness and conditional hedging features.

Modeling the FX Rate

I modeled the FX spot rate \( S_t \) using two stochastic processes:

  • Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM): The classical Black-Scholes model under a risk-neutral measure.
  • Heston Model: A stochastic volatility model that better captures empirical phenomena like volatility clustering and leverage effects.

Sample paths of the FX spot rate under geometric Brownian motion used in Monte Carlo simulation.

Using Monte Carlo simulation, I generated thousands of synthetic price paths and computed expected payoffs. However, simulating barrier options na√Øvely introduces significant variance and discretization bias, especially when monitoring is discrete (e.g. daily or hourly).

Variance Reduction and Interpolation

To improve estimator efficiency, I incorporated several techniques:

  • Antithetic Variates: For each simulated path, I also simulate its antithetic complement (by negating the Brownian motion), and average their payoffs to reduce variance.
  • Control Variates: I use a closely related option with known expected value (like a European call) as a control.
  • Brownian Bridge Interpolation: Since discrete sampling can miss barrier crossings, the bridge helps estimate whether the barrier was crossed between time steps.

Two FX paths (dashed) that survive discrete monitoring yet likely breach the barrier when interpolated. Brownian bridge adjustment penalizes these cases.

Results

Simulation under GBM gave reasonable estimates but struggled in scenarios with low volatility or tight barriers. The variance reduction techniques dramatically improved convergence and reduced standard error. Brownian bridge interpolation made a measurable difference in correctly classifying knock-outs.

The Heston model added computational cost but yielded more realistic dynamics, especially for FX options. It captured ‚Äúfat tails‚Äù and volatility smiles that GBM couldn‚Äôt replicate. I ran empirical tests comparing both models across multiple scenarios and found clear tradeoffs between realism and simplicity.
